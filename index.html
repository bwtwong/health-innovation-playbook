Maturity Scale
Technology Readiness Levels (TRL)
A 9-point scale from raw concept to proven product. The universal language for "how far along is this, really?"
NASA OriginMaturityFunding Gates

The 9-level scale

9System proven in operational environment
8System complete and qualified
7System prototype in operational environment
6Technology demonstrated in relevant environment
5Technology validated in relevant environment
4Technology validated in lab
3Experimental proof of concept
2Technology concept formulated
1Basic principles observed

Why it matters commercially

Investors, grant bodies, and corporate partners use TRL as a shorthand risk indicator. TRL 1–3 is academic/grant-funded territory. TRL 4–6 is the riskiest and hardest-to-fund "Valley of Death" zone. TRL 7–9 is where commercial investors re-engage because execution risk (not technical risk) dominates.

How to use it in practice

  1. Be brutally honest about current TRL — overclaiming kills credibility with investors and HTA bodies
  2. Identify exactly what evidence is needed to move up one level
  3. Match funding source to TRL: grants for TRL 1–4, venture/strategic for TRL 5+
  4. For health tech, layer TRL against Clinical Readiness Level (CRL) — they are not the same axis

Common pitfalls

  • Treating TRL as linear and irreversible — failed validation can knock a technology back a level
  • Confusing engineering readiness (TRL) with clinical or regulatory readiness (CRL) — a device can be TRL 8 but CRL 3
  • Assuming TRL 9 means commercially viable — it only means technically proven, not adopted or funded

Maturity Scale
Clinical Readiness Levels (CRL)
TRL's health-specific cousin — tracking clinical validation, regulatory approval, and post-market surveillance separately from engineering maturity.
MedTechClinical TrialsHealth-Specific

The CRL stages

9Post-market surveillance & continual safety monitoring
8Regulatory approval / market authorisation achieved
7Pivotal/confirmatory clinical trial complete
6Pivotal trial design validated (Phase II/III equivalent)
5Early feasibility/pilot clinical study complete
4First-in-human / proof-of-concept study
3Pre-clinical safety and efficacy testing (animal/in-vitro)
2Clinical concept and regulatory strategy defined
1Clinical need identified

Why two scales, not one

A device can be engineering-mature (TRL 9 — works flawlessly as a machine) but clinically immature (CRL 3 — never been tested for safety or efficacy in patients). Investors and HTA bodies care about CRL far more than TRL once a product nears market.

Application in Australia

  1. CRL 1–4: typically funded by MRFF, NHMRC grants, university tech transfer offices
  2. CRL 5–6: requires Human Research Ethics Committee (HREC) approval, often via National Mutual Acceptance (NMA) for multi-site trials
  3. CRL 7–8: TGA submission and ARTG listing pathway begins
  4. CRL 8–9: parallel/post-ARTG, MSAC or PBAC submission for MBS/PBS funding

The investor lens

  • Health-focused VCs typically won't invest meaningfully before CRL 4–5 (some clinical signal exists)
  • The biggest valuation step-change occurs at CRL 7→8 (regulatory approval de-risks dramatically)
  • CRL 8 ≠ revenue — reimbursement (MBS/PBS listing) is a separate, often slower gate

Funding Strategy
The Valley of Death
Why most health innovations die between grant funding and commercial investment — and how to plan around it.
Funding GapTranslationRisk

What it is

The Valley of Death describes the funding gap between early-stage research (typically grant-funded, TRL 1–4 / CRL 1–4) and late-stage commercialisation (typically venture or strategic-funded, TRL 7+ / CRL 6+). In the middle, neither grant bodies nor commercial investors want to fund the work — it's too applied for academic grants, too risky for venture capital.

Why this gap exists

  • Grant funders (NHMRC, MRFF) fund discovery and early proof-of-concept, not product development
  • Venture investors want de-risked clinical and regulatory signal before committing capital
  • The work in between — prototyping, pilot trials, regulatory strategy — is expensive and high-risk with no clear funder mandate

Bridging strategies

  1. Target translational funding specifically designed for this gap (MRFF Translational Research Grants, BioMedTech Horizons, Frontier Health and Medical Research)
  2. Seek non-dilutive grants from MTPConnect or state government innovation funds (e.g. WA's Innovation Booster)
  3. Pursue strategic corporate partnerships early — pharma/medtech co-development de-risks both technical and commercial uncertainty simultaneously
  4. Build a syndicate of angel/pre-seed investors comfortable with binary clinical risk
  5. Sequence milestones to create natural "proof points" that justify staged funding asks

Strategic implication for founders

  • Map your funding plan to specific TRL/CRL milestones before you need the money
  • The Valley of Death is a planning problem, not just bad luck — most failures here were predictable
  • Consider de-risking sequence: which is cheaper to prove first, clinical signal or regulatory pathway clarity?

Commercialisation Process
Stage-Gate Process
Structured go/no-go decisions at each phase of development — preventing good money chasing bad science.
Cooper ModelDecision GatesNPD

The standard stages

  • Stage 0 — Discovery: Idea generation, unmet need identification
  • Stage 1 — Scoping: Preliminary market and technical assessment
  • Stage 2 — Business Case: Detailed investigation, regulatory strategy, IP position
  • Stage 3 — Development: Product/prototype development, pre-clinical work
  • Stage 4 — Validation: Clinical trials, regulatory submission
  • Stage 5 — Launch: Market entry, reimbursement, commercial scale-up

The gates

Between each stage sits a formal "gate" — a structured go/kill/hold/recycle decision made against pre-defined criteria (technical feasibility, clinical evidence strength, market size, regulatory risk, financial viability). Gates exist specifically to kill bad projects early, before they consume more capital.

Why health needs strict gates

  1. Health development is capital-intensive and slow — sunk cost bias is dangerous
  2. Each gate should have explicit kill criteria agreed before the stage begins, not after results are seen
  3. Gatekeepers should include people with no emotional investment in the project's survival
  4. "Recycle" (send back for more evidence) is a legitimate outcome, not just go/kill

Common organisational failure

  • Gates become rubber stamps — everything proceeds regardless of evidence
  • No one owns the kill decision — diffused accountability protects bad projects
  • Gate criteria aren't quantified, so "feels promising" substitutes for evidence

Australian Regulation
TGA Regulatory Pathway
Before anything can be sold in Australia, it must clear the Therapeutic Goods Administration. Here's the route.
TGAARTGClass I-III

What the TGA does

The Therapeutic Goods Administration regulates the safety, quality, and efficacy of therapeutic goods (medicines, medical devices, biologicals) supplied in Australia. Market authorisation means listing on the Australian Register of Therapeutic Goods (ARTG) — a separate process to reimbursement.

Device classification (risk-based)

  • Class I: Low risk (e.g. bandages, simple instruments) — self-certification often sufficient
  • Class IIa/IIb: Medium risk (e.g. infusion pumps, hearing aids) — requires conformity assessment
  • Class III: High risk (e.g. implantable devices) — requires full clinical evidence and TGA conformity assessment
  • Classification closely mirrors the EU MDR system — a CE mark often supports the Australian pathway via the Overseas Comparable Assessment (OCA) route

The core steps to ARTG listing

  1. Determine device classification using the TGA Medical Device Classification Tool
  2. Obtain manufacturer conformity certification (TGA or recognised overseas regulator, e.g. EU CE mark)
  3. Submit ARTG application with the manufacturer's certificate (for Class II+)
  4. Comply with Essential Principles (safety, performance, labelling, UDI requirements)
  5. Post-market: ongoing pharmacovigilance/technovigilance reporting obligations

Practical tips

  • A pre-submission meeting with TGA is available and strongly recommended for novel technologies
  • Leveraging an existing CE mark or FDA clearance significantly accelerates the Australian pathway
  • TGA approval (ARTG listing) does not mean the product is funded — that's a separate MSAC/PBAC/MBS/PBS process

Australian Reimbursement
Health Technology Assessment: PBAC & MSAC
Regulatory approval gets you to market. HTA gets you paid. The two committees that decide if government will fund your innovation.
PBACMSACCost-Effectiveness

The two committees

  • PBAC (Pharmaceutical Benefits Advisory Committee): Assesses medicines for PBS (Pharmaceutical Benefits Scheme) listing. Cannot recommend listing unless satisfied the medicine represents value for money vs existing therapies.
  • MSAC (Medical Services Advisory Committee): The equivalent body for medical devices and diagnostic services, assessing clinical and cost-effectiveness for MBS (Medicare Benefits Schedule) funding.

The core evaluation logic

Both committees use Incremental Cost-Effectiveness Ratio (ICER) = Change in Cost ÷ Change in QALY (quality-adjusted life years), benchmarked against standard of care or "doing nothing." A lower ICER means more health gain per dollar — more likely to be funded.

Practical pathway

  1. PBAC meets 3 times per year (March, July, November) — plan submissions around this cycle
  2. Standard process takes minimum ~6 months, longer with price negotiation
  3. TGA-PBAC parallel process available for Category 1/2 submissions — runs regulatory and reimbursement assessment simultaneously, saving significant time
  4. A positive recommendation triggers price negotiation between sponsor and Department of Health for the Approved Ex-Manufacturer Price (AEMP)
  5. Rejections can be resubmitted or independently reviewed

For codependent technologies (drug + diagnostic)

  • Where a drug requires a companion diagnostic, submissions can be integrated (joint MSAC + PBAC) or parallel (streamlined)
  • Integrated submissions take longer but ensure alignment; parallel is faster but riskier if the two assessments diverge

Funding Strategy
Reimbursement Pathway Mapping
There's no single way to get paid in Australian health. Map the right pathway before you build the product.
MBSPBSProstheses List

The major funding pathways in Australia

  • PBS: Subsidised medicines — via PBAC recommendation
  • MBS: Subsidised medical services/procedures — via MSAC recommendation
  • Prescribed List (formerly Prostheses List): Mandatory minimum benefit for implants/prostheses that private health insurers must cover
  • State health budgets: Hospital-based technologies often procured directly by state health systems, bypassing Commonwealth HTA
  • Private health insurance (beyond Prescribed List): Negotiated separately with individual funds
  • Out-of-pocket / direct-to-consumer: No reimbursement pathway — relies on consumer willingness to pay

The strategic question every founder must answer early

"Who is actually going to pay for this, and through which mechanism?" Many promising health technologies fail not on clinical grounds but because no clear reimbursement pathway exists — they fall between Commonwealth, state, and private funding structures.

Mapping approach

  1. Identify the most analogous existing item already funded (comparator) — this anchors your pricing and evidence strategy
  2. Determine whether your technology is a medicine, device, service, or digital health product — each has a different committee
  3. Map state vs Commonwealth funding boundaries — hospital-based vs community-based technologies are funded very differently
  4. Consider hybrid strategies: direct-to-consumer revenue while pursuing reimbursement in parallel

Digital health complication

  • Digital therapeutics and AI software often don't fit neatly into existing MBS item descriptors
  • New MBS items require MSAC application — a slow, evidence-intensive process
  • Many digital health companies pursue B2B2C models (selling to hospitals/insurers directly) to bypass individual reimbursement uncertainty

Evidence Generation
Health Economics & Outcomes Research (HEOR)
The discipline of proving your innovation is worth what it costs — the evidence engine behind every HTA submission.
QALYBurden of DiseaseModelling

Core HEOR concepts

  • QALY (Quality-Adjusted Life Year): One year of life at perfect health = 1 QALY. Combines length and quality of life into a single comparable metric.
  • ICER: Incremental Cost-Effectiveness Ratio — extra cost per extra QALY gained vs the comparator
  • Burden of Disease: The total health impact of a condition, often measured in Disability-Adjusted Life Years (DALYs)
  • Budget Impact Analysis: The total cost to the health system of funding the new technology at scale — distinct from cost-effectiveness

The three pillars of an HEOR case

  1. Clinical evidence: Does it work? (efficacy, safety — usually from trials)
  2. Economic evidence: Is it worth the cost? (cost-effectiveness modelling, ICER)
  3. Real-world evidence: Does it work outside controlled trial conditions? (registries, observational data)

Building the economic model

  • Choose your comparator carefully — usually current standard of care, sometimes "no intervention"
  • Decision-tree models for short-term outcomes; Markov models for chronic, long-term conditions
  • Sensitivity analysis is mandatory — show how the ICER changes under different assumptions
  • Perspective matters: health system perspective (most common in Australia) vs societal perspective (includes productivity, carer burden)

Practical advice for founders

  • Engage a health economist early — HEOR strategy should shape your clinical trial design, not follow it
  • Budget impact concerns can kill a clinically excellent technology — a $200/QALY ICER means nothing if total system cost is unaffordable
  • Real-world evidence registries can be planned from day one of commercial launch to support future re-submissions

Evidence Strategy
The Evidence Hierarchy
Not all evidence is equal. Knowing what level of proof your claim requires saves years and millions.
RCTStudy DesignNHMRC

The hierarchy (strongest to weakest)

  • Systematic review / meta-analysis of randomised controlled trials
  • Randomised controlled trial (RCT) — gold standard for causal claims
  • Cohort study — observes groups over time, no randomisation
  • Case-control study — compares those with/without the outcome retrospectively
  • Case series / case reports — descriptive, no comparison group
  • Expert opinion — lowest level, useful for hypothesis generation only

Matching evidence to claim

The level of evidence required scales with the claim's risk and reimbursement stakes. A wellness app claiming "may support relaxation" needs minimal evidence. A device claiming "reduces hospital readmission by 25%" for MBS funding needs RCT-level evidence at minimum.

Strategic evidence planning

  1. Identify your core commercial claim before designing any study
  2. Work backwards: what evidence level does that claim require for your target reimbursement pathway?
  3. Design the minimum viable study that generates that evidence level — don't over- or under-engineer
  4. Consider adaptive trial designs to reduce cost and timeline where regulators allow

Real-world evidence's growing role

  • TGA and HTA bodies increasingly accept real-world evidence (RWE) to supplement RCT data, especially post-market
  • RWE cannot usually replace RCT evidence for initial reimbursement decisions, but strengthens re-submissions and expanded indications

The Clinical Adoption Pathway
Clinical approval ≠ clinical adoption. Why technically superior health technologies still fail to get used.
CliniciansWorkflowDiffusion

The adoption gap

Unlike consumer products, health innovation adoption depends on a complex web of clinician trust, workflow integration, institutional procurement, and patient acceptance — all separate from clinical and regulatory approval. A technology can be TGA-approved, MBS-funded, and clinically superior, and still fail to achieve meaningful uptake.

The key adoption barriers in health

  • Workflow disruption: Does it add steps to an already time-pressured clinical workflow?
  • Clinician trust: Has it been validated by peers, professional colleges, or guidelines?
  • Liability concerns: Who is responsible if the technology contributes to an adverse outcome?
  • Institutional procurement: Hospital purchasing committees, formularies, IT security review
  • Patient trust and digital literacy: Especially relevant for self-managed digital health tools

Strategies for adoption

  1. Identify and cultivate clinical champions early — peer endorsement drives uptake more than marketing
  2. Design for minimal workflow disruption — integrate into existing systems (EHR, ordering systems) rather than requiring separate logins/processes
  3. Pursue professional college and guideline endorsement where possible
  4. Pilot in a small number of high-credibility sites before broad rollout — create reference sites
  5. Address liability explicitly in product design and contracts, not as an afterthought

The "last mile" problem

  • Most health-tech failures happen post-approval, in the adoption phase, not the development phase
  • Budget and resource for adoption support (training, change management, helpdesk) as seriously as you budget for clinical trials

Health GTM Channel Strategy
B2C, B2B, or B2B2C — choosing the right channel determines your entire business model, sales cycle, and capital needs.
ChannelsB2B2CSales Cycle

The three channel models

  • B2C (direct to patient/consumer): Fast to launch, no reimbursement dependency, but requires consumer willingness-to-pay and high marketing spend
  • B2B (direct to institution): Hospitals, health systems, insurers as customers. Long sales cycles (6–18 months), but larger contract values and stickier relationships
  • B2B2C (institution as intermediary to patient): Most common in health tech — sell to a hospital/insurer/employer who then provides it to patients. Combines institutional trust with patient-level engagement requirements

Choosing the right model

The right channel depends on who experiences the value, who controls the budget, and who bears the regulatory/clinical risk. If clinical risk is high, B2B or B2B2C is usually necessary — institutions provide governance and liability infrastructure that direct-to-consumer cannot.

Sales cycle realities in Australian health

  1. Hospital procurement typically involves clinical governance committees, IT security review, and finance sign-off — budget 6–18 months
  2. State health systems often require separate procurement processes per state — a national rollout means multiple sales cycles, not one
  3. Private health insurers move faster than public hospitals but require clear cost-offset evidence
  4. Primary care (GP clinics) has shorter cycles but lower individual contract value — requires volume

Capital implications

  • B2B2C models require capital runway to survive long institutional sales cycles before revenue materialises
  • Consider hybrid sequencing: B2C or direct-pay revenue to fund operations while pursuing slower B2B/B2B2C deals

IP Strategy
IP Strategy for Health Innovation
No investor funds a health technology without a defensible IP position. Patents, freedom to operate, and the licensing decision.
PatentsFTOLicensing

Core IP concepts

  • Patent: Exclusive right to exclude others from making/using/selling the invention (typically 20 years)
  • Freedom to Operate (FTO): Confirmation that your product doesn't infringe on someone else's existing patents — distinct from having your own patent
  • Trade secret: Protecting know-how through confidentiality rather than disclosure (useful for processes, algorithms not easily reverse-engineered)
  • Licensing: Granting another party rights to use your IP, typically for royalties or upfront payment

The critical FTO mistake

Founders often assume "we have a patent" means they can freely sell their product. It doesn't. You can hold a valid patent on your specific innovation while still infringing someone else's broader patent in operating your product. FTO analysis is a separate, essential exercise.

Build vs license vs partner decision

  1. Build (in-house IP): Maximum control and upside, but requires capital and expertise to develop and defend
  2. License in: Faster path to market using existing IP, but ongoing royalty obligations and less differentiation
  3. License out: Monetise university/research IP without bearing full commercialisation risk and cost
  4. Strategic partnership: Co-development with a larger player, sharing IP and risk

For university spin-outs specifically

  • Most Australian universities retain background IP — spin-outs typically negotiate an exclusive licence, not outright ownership
  • Tech transfer office (TTO) terms (royalty rates, equity stakes, milestone payments) should be negotiated early — they significantly affect future investability
  • Publication timing must be carefully managed against patent filing — public disclosure before filing can destroy patentability in most jurisdictions

Commercialisation Process
Technology Transfer Process
Moving an innovation from a university lab into a commercial entity — the structured pathway and the typical pitfalls.
UniversitySpin-outTTO

The typical process

  1. Invention disclosure to the university's Tech Transfer Office (TTO)
  2. TTO assesses commercial potential and patentability
  3. Patent filing (provisional, then full application within 12 months)
  4. Commercialisation pathway decision: licence to existing company, or spin out a new company
  5. If spin-out: negotiate founder equity, university equity stake, and licence terms
  6. Seek translational funding to progress TRL/CRL toward commercial readiness

Key stakeholders to align

  • Inventor(s): Often academic staff who may stay involved as scientific advisors but not full-time operators
  • University TTO: Negotiates equity/royalty terms, manages IP filing
  • Incoming CEO/management: Often brought in externally — academic founders frequently aren't the right operational leaders
  • External investors: Will scrutinise the IP/equity structure closely before committing capital

Common friction points

  • University equity/royalty demands that are too aggressive for future investors to accept — gets renegotiated, wasting time and goodwill
  • Conflict between academic publication incentives and commercial confidentiality needs
  • Founding academic insisting on CEO role despite lacking commercial experience
  • Underestimating the capital and time needed between disclosure and product readiness

Australian ecosystem support

  • MTPConnect, BioMedTech Horizons, and CSIRO's Innovation Fund all provide translational support
  • State-based programs (e.g. WA's Innovation Booster, NSW Medical Devices Fund) offer early-stage non-dilutive grants
  • University accelerators and incubators (often co-located with TTOs) provide structured commercialisation support
